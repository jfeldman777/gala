The scale of ideal forms splits all our ideas into 8 classes, and each class has its own quality criteria.

1. Events — records — completeness and precision of detail matter
2. Facts — narratives — clarity and persuasiveness of cause-and-effect links matter
3. Objects — biographies — continuity and definiteness matter
4. Generalizations — patterns — recognizability and frequency of occurrence matter
5. Methods — USEFULNESS matters, along with predictability, error tolerance, and entry threshold (Technology)
6. Values — GENERAL ACCEPTANCE matters, along with openness to the future (Art)
7. Views — OBVIOUSNESS matters, along with openness to the future and non-conflict with science (Philosophy)
8. Knowledge — ADEQUACY TO THE WORLD, SELF-CONSISTENCY; must withstand rigorous scientific testing — the P-Procedure (Science) — models = ideal constructs, whose purpose is to be the core of specific knowledge

Notice the words written entirely in capital letters. Recognize them? That's Baumeister's list!

Now it's clear that these are quality criteria applicable to different classes. That's how we resolved the first objection.

Note that these criteria aren't the only ones in each class. Without the rest of the criteria on the list, they don't work! And if they contradict the rest of the criteria on the list, they should be sacrificed, while the rest of the criteria are kept.

A generally accepted value can be reactionary (like slavery).
Obviousness can contradict science (the sun rises in the east).

Adequacy and self-consistency aren't enough for science.

We've just solved Baumeister's problem of justifying the series for him. His list isn't criteria of truth — it's markers of quality within different classes; moreover, the markers don't cover the full set of criteria for a class, and when they conflict with other criteria they must yield.

First conclusion. There is no Truth — there is truthfulness or falsity for each class of ideal forms.

Second conclusion. Baumeister, along with all of official philosophy, has stumbled here.
