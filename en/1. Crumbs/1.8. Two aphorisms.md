Many years ago, when after university I found myself a young specialist in Zelenograd, we had sessions with a mentor.
Two aphorisms from those sessions stuck with me.
1. Give the client not what he asks for, but what he needs.
2. Can a machine replace a human? Depends which human. Some can be replaced by a hammer.

Let's try to analyze these two aphorisms.

## The First Aphorism
There's you, and there's the client.
The client has a problem. He knows, in theory, how to solve it. And he needs help implementing his solution. He asks for that help.

What does the aphorism claim?
It claims that the client doesn't fully understand the problem in front of him. And that you can understand this problem more deeply than the client does. And that you shouldn't implement the client's solution, but your own — and in such a way that he'll like it.

This implicitly assumes that you're smarter than the client.
It doesn't say he's stupid — he's probably quite smart.
But your mind is smarter than his mind.
In other words, intelligence has degrees.
