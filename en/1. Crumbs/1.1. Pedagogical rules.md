These are the rules by which Elena Nikolaevna — my wife-to-be — ran her classes in Moscow in 1988. They give a sense of what good pedagogy should look like.
## 1.      The Speaker's Rule

Every thought a student states must begin

either with the words

"I know that..." (in which case one can ask — "How do you know that?" — this trains the habit of remembering one's sources)

or with the words

"I think that..." (in which case it's clear there's nowhere to get the knowledge from, and a guess must be built) — and this trains the courage to state one's own opinion and the skill of building hypotheses.

Telling the first apart from the second is an important part of analytical thinking; training the first and the second separately is a useful skill.
## 2.      The Artist's Rule

If a class ends with drawing, then at the end all the drawings are hung up around the perimeter of the room. Each student — together with the teacher (and a parent, if present) — goes around the exhibition in such a way that their own work ends up being the last one.

About other people's work, the person going around must say: "What did I like here?"

About their own: "What would I improve?"

This removes rivalry,
builds a positive attitude toward other people's creative work,
and a readiness to improve one's own creative output.

The evaluations come from the children themselves, which removes the danger of unrealistic, inflated demands from the teacher (after which — if that happens — the child may stop drawing altogether).
## 3.      The Memory Rule

At the end of a class, the teacher asks the children to draw what they remember.

From the content of the drawing, the teacher sees what was remembered and what wasn't,

can judge the degree of accuracy and the degree of creativity,

and from the structure of the drawing, the teacher gets information about the child's level of thinking (mental age).

## 4.      Discussion Order

Usually, at the start of a new topic, the teacher shows an object or an image of an object, and asks a question (What is this? What is it for?).

Everyone must speak. In a mixed-age group, the younger ones speak first, then the older ones (for this, before the class begins, the participants are seated around the circle in the appropriate order).

## 5.      The Rule of Joining In

It's allowed to join the opinion of the previous participant.

Conversely, repeating what's already been said (instead of joining in) is considered a sign of inattention — one should have been listening to what others say, and not waste other participants' time with a pointless repetition.

If there's something to add, it must be added.

## 6.      The Rule of Ascent

The teacher runs joint activity across all levels, starting from the first (at the start — showing a thing or an image of a single object).

Not every material lets you move organically through all the levels.

The history of culture does.

If the culture is ancient, the upper levels are built up through the tasks of modern research into ancient life.

Everyone starts from the first level — moving upward lets each person stop at the limit of their own abilities.

This rules out the situation where the teacher limits the complexity of a student's activity (and thereby holds back their development).

## 7.      The Group-Size Rule

For everyone to be able to speak, and for everyone to hear everyone, the number of participants may not exceed 9 people, including the adults. If, with the parents, there would be more, the parents sit in a second circle and don't take part in the discussion.

## 8.      Warm-up

Since a class assumes that "everyone hears everyone," which rarely happens with children in ordinary life, about 15 minutes before the class there is a tuning-in period — the teacher runs the children through special exercises for managing attention, concentration, coordination, and interaction.

## 9.      The Group Closes

Experience in managing attention gradually builds up. And a moment comes when a new person can no longer be added to the group. If classes happen often — twice a week or more — this moment arrives after a month. If classes happen once a week — after a month and a half. Meeting less often than that isn't allowed.

After this moment, it's practically impossible to bring a new student into the group. If one is brought in anyway, their lag behind the group will be a PERMANENT source of conflict.

## 10. No Sitting Out

This applies to the adults who bring the children. They must take part in the class on equal footing with the children. It's important to make sure the adult works on their own and doesn't interfere with the child's work or statements (they answer for themselves — and you answer for yourself).

## 11. The "Kangaroo Pouch" Rule

If a child comes with an adult, the adult takes part in the class as a student. The small child sits on the adult's lap, and the child-adult pair takes part in the discussion as a single person.

Sooner or later a moment will come when the child "climbs out of the kangaroo pouch" and starts working on their own.

## 12. The Space Rule

The furniture and the space must be comfortable for children and adults at the same time.

## 13. The Rule of Free Movement

If the material is currently too hard, a child may move over to the toddlers' area and play. After a while they usually come back on their own with the question, "So what's going on here?" The right to choose (here or there?) stays with the child.
