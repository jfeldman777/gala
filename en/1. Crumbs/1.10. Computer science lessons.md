In my life I worked twice as a school teacher, two years each time.
Including as a computer science teacher.
I take the school curriculum — and what do I see.

Part of the material is simply wrong — it's outdated.
Part of the material everyone already knows.
Part of the material is impossible to understand, in principle.
Part of the material is hard and is clearly needed only by specialists.
Part of the material can't be tested at all — you can't tell someone who knows it from someone who doesn't.

Whatever is left should be turned into a series of tasks, so that a person can get a practical result and show it off. Within a reasonable amount of time, with reasonable effort.

My result as a teacher.

At the start of the year, ONLY two girls were working.
By the end of the year, ONLY one boy was NOT working.

One more thought.
Children who come to your lesson and then run off to the next one are automata without memory. They must solve their task without relying on knowledge from previous lessons.

It's precisely the teacher's attempt to rely on prior knowledge that causes children, by the end of the year, to STOP learning (with me — see above — they STARTED learning).

Hence, the organizing principle of this book. Any fragment of the book must be understandable on its own, to about 90%. And to get to 100%, you need to read and understand the WHOLE book.
