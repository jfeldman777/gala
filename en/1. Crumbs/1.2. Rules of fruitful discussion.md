1. Hold the term. There are no synonyms.
2. The Lone Zombie — a lone concept is dead; living concepts travel in series.
3. The task in the background. While people on the foreground talk beautifully, in the background a task must be hanging. If our conversations don't make the task clearer, we're wasting time for nothing.
4. Every new series must (in its author's own words) answer four questions (Justification of the series)
	1. Is the series homogeneous?
	2. Is the series complete? (incomplete series can be useful too)
	3. Is the order unique? (a non-unique order can be useful too)
	4. Where did this series come from?
	   The burden of justification lies with the series' author.
5. Structuring. We will inevitably run into continuous, infinitely fine scales of values. Since we work for a person making decisions within a limited window of time and possibility, it is not just our right but our duty to replace the infinite with the finite — continuous scales are thereby turned into finite sequences (series).
6. Reverse engineering. If you're unhappy with someone else's idea, find out whether it's part of the problem statement, or part of the solution. In the second case, find the problem. Examine the problem. If it's not your problem, forget it. If it is, give a good solution.
7. Self-applicability. Since a theory of thinking is itself an act of thinking, the constraints this theory imposes on others it must also apply to itself. Example: the justification of a series must itself be justified. These eight rules are also a series, and it too must be justified.
8. Openness forever. A solved problem can be declared unsolved again if there are new ideas and new data.

Effective thinking saves time.
