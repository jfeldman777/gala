Maslow's student was Clare William Graves.
He idolized his teacher and began nearly every article with the phrase, "The theory I use is a slightly improved version of Maslow's theory."
It's said that at the academic council where Maslow first presented his pyramid, senior colleagues came down on him hard. But the pyramid lives on — who remembers the names of those colleagues now?

Graves, as Maslow's graduate student, stood behind the door and eavesdropped through the not-quite-closed gap. The attack by the senior colleagues impressed him so much that for the rest of his life he kept delaying and postponing publications that were already finished, and in his final years, seriously ill, he took part in nothing at all.

After Graves's death in 1987, not a single book of his remained — only articles and seminar notes, which his student Chris Cowan was preparing for publication. Preparing them, unhurriedly... But then, in 1995, a man named Don Beck returned to America from South Africa. He happened to come across Graves's theory and decided it could make a pile of money. And in 1996, Don Beck and Chris Cowan published a book called Spiral Dynamics. Graves's own writings came out as a separate book in 2005.

Ken Wilber read the book — and folded the model it described into his own pantheon of assorted models.

When I published "The Theory of Levels and the Model of Man" in 2003, one of my readers pointed me toward Spiral Dynamics.

And I discovered that, phenomenologically, my levels and the Graves-Beck levels are the very same levels! Though they never had any theory of their own to answer the question — why is that so. In other words, I had a full sandwich, while they only had an open one.
