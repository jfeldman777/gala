We all understand the difference between religion and science, between religion and myth, and finally between science and myth.

But how can we define this difference?

I see three criteria.

The first is **historical**.
Historical societies were first dominated by myth, then by religion, and finally by science.

The second criterion is **structural**. Myth has no author, no professionals, and no canon; religion has professionals and a canon, but no authors. Science has professionals, an author, and a canon that drifts slightly.

The third criterion is **divine**. Myth is polytheistic, religion is monotheistic, science is godless.

I will show that these three regularities are connected — not in the sense that one generates another, but in the sense that all of them grow from a common root.

So, some information circulates in society. Will it become science, religion, or myth?

And what does that depend on?

I think it depends on the maturity of society. In what sense? In this sense.
The word "circulates" implies cycles. But I will assume something else. There is a directed graph whose vertices are people and whose edges are the paths along which information spreads.

In the general case, such a graph has source vertices, which no edges enter; sink vertices, from which it is impossible to leave; and all the rest.
Let's call the rest "through" vertices.

Recall that the vertices are people. If these people create new information, they are Authors. If they pass it on further, they are Transmitters. If they only receive it, they are Consumers.

My hypothesis is that a person, while growing up, strives to take one of these three possible positions. And once they take it, they live in it until death.

In the chapter on exams and automata, we discussed the possible tasks on a written exam — **dictation, summary, essay, and free-form essay.**

I ordered these forms so that the earlier ones are easier than the later ones.

If you need a consumer, the best exam is a dictation.

For a transmitter — a summary.

For an author — an essay.

I also assume that no training is needed to become a consumer.
But training is needed to become a transmitter — and an exam must be passed.
And to become an author, even more training is needed, and an even harder exam must be passed.

I further assumed that humanity keeps getting smarter.

What does "getting smarter" mean?

It means that in earlier stages, no one aspired to pass an exam and become a certified transmitter. Even less did anyone aspire to be recognized as an author. Officially, everyone was considered a consumer. So there was no authorship, no canon (which is kept by professionals — and there weren't any yet). This is the age of Myth.

Centuries passed. People grew bolder and smarter. A certain number of the brave-and-smart appeared who were ready to pass an exam and become transmitters. They were declared professional transmitters. Now they had to preserve the canon. The result was religion.

More centuries passed, people grew even smarter and bolder, and enough people appeared who were ready to pass the exam for Author. There are professionals, and there is a canon. But since Authorship implies creativity, the canon gradually started to drift. This is science.

Why does religion have a God, while science does not? Because in the age of religion, Authorship is forbidden to humans. But a product is still needed, Authors are still needed, yet they cannot come out into God's light — for that is forbidden. So who is the Author, then? A superhuman, a superbeing, God.

And why polytheism before religion, while religion is usually monotheistic? Because myth has no canon, and everyone invents their own god.
