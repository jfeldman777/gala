For every elementary message, one can find an author, transmitters, and consumers.

The spread of messages = communication.

Now — what goals does a participant in communication set for themselves?

Here are some options:
1) Co-knowledge (joint cognition) — I tell you something in order to find out how you will assess it, and — possibly — we will plan a joint action about it together.
2) Unifying contexts. I know something, and I want you to know it too — the larger the shared context, the easier it is to communicate.
3) Management — a boss issues an order, or a rector issues a new instruction.
4) Manipulation — I tell others false information in order to provoke an action that benefits me and harms others.

Management is almost indistinguishable from any other communication — the main difference is sanctions for failing to comply with, or deviating from, the order or instruction.

We are only interested in the measure of the participant's activity. And it is distributed the same way in every case.
