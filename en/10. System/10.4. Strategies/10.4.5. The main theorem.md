# The main idea

So, in society there are positions of three types with respect to decisions being made.

1. Consumers
2. Transmitters
3. Authors

And everyone who enters society must take one of these positions.

But here is an interesting observation. Such a stratification of society implies viewing society as 1) side by side, 2) trees, 3) oriented from the roots.

Aren't we oversimplifying? [In the chapter The Basic Question of Philosophy (24)](https://app.notion.com/p/24-1db22db3cb0880c4b292c0447a45bcbf?pvs=21) we showed that there are, in total, four types of complexity in directed graphs (and here we used only 2).

But the other two kinds (many bosses, and a cycle) are not growing in modern society, and their share of cases is small (although they do cause real problems for real people).

Let's set aside complexity #3 (isolated vertices) for now, and we are left with one large tree, oriented from the root.

Look around and you will see many such trees. Now let's look more closely, and we will find that such hierarchies come in two qualitatively different types.

In type A hierarchies, specific decisions spread, working here and now. This resembles an Army, though many corporations and even states run on this model.

In type B hierarchies, general ideas, rules, and formulas spread.

This resembles science — although religions and ideologies work the same way.

It turns out that where above we considered three positions, we need to consider six!

Imagine a school in front of you — a school building with eight classrooms. The school trains students under eight programs. Each room always has the same program. Naturally, the teachers will accumulate suitable materials, and over many years they will accumulate a lot.

Here are these six positions (soldier, officer, general, student, instructor, textbook author) — six niches where candidates for positions are prepared, and where six types of tasks accumulate.

These were the static strategies — but there are also dynamic ones.
