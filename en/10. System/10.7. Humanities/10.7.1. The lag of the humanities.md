Compare the news from the natural sciences (including mathematics) with the news from the humanities (including philosophy).

Compare their practical usefulness.

Notice how, starting with Kant, scholars in the humanities regularly apologize "for their unscientific nature."

In English, Science refers only to the natural sciences. For the humanities, people say Study, that is, an inquiry with an unknown result.

Here are a few reasons why the humanities lag behind.
1) Little data. Experiments are difficult. Surveys are unreliable.
2) Fragmentation. Almost no one uses other people's data — everyone gathers their own from scratch.
3) Untranslatable languages, cooperation is impossible.

Clearly, this last point produces the previous ones. But what is its cause?

The cause is that a theoretical model and the data are born together and die together. Too tight a link between theory and experiment rules out transferring the model to other experiments.

Humanities scholars do not distinguish between a theoretical construct and an empirical generalization.
A physicist will never confuse a ball with a point mass. A humanities scholar easily will.

Where does this tight coupling come from? From Aristotle.

Recall Aristotle's physics and Democritus's physics.
And the decisive "nature abhors a vacuum."

Recall also Aristotle's polemic with Plato.
For understanding the world of things, ideas as a construct are not needed.
If ideas are introduced to explain things, then second-order ideas are needed to explain the ideas, and so on ad infinitum. A vicious infinite regress.

But let's apply this argument of Aristotle's to the Kepler–Newton sandwich.
Tycho Brahe gathers numbers.
Kepler sees formulas behind the numbers — IDEAS.

Newton sets out to explain these ideas and introduces the three laws of mechanics and the law of gravitation — second-order ideas.
Do Newton's laws need to be explained? One could say Einstein explained them. And who will explain Einstein?

Nevertheless, no vicious infinite regress resulted — modern physics resulted.

Conclusions.
1. The humanities have fallen 400 years behind the natural sciences — Galileo freed the latter, I am freeing the former.
2. We need to catch up — build unified models, pool data, cooperate, and obtain practical results.
