Recall the model "A person in a stream of tasks." One interpretation: a student at an oral exam receives similar problems at a steady pace and tries to solve them. As this happens, the speed and accuracy of solving change. At first they rise, "the person gets into the groove," then they are stable, and finally they fall, "the person gets tired."
![[Pasted image 20260714094940.png|517]]

I suggest that behind the observable characteristics "accuracy" and "speed" there is one characteristic that is not directly observable, which I call "capacity for work." This means that, in the general case, this characteristic changes over time as shown in the figure.

![[Pasted image 20260714093604.png]]
Now let's sit different students side by side and compare them. From my observations, their warm-up curves fall into 4 types.

![[Pasted image 20260714093650.png]]
1. Rises quickly, flies high, but not for long, descends steeply.
2. Rises more slowly, doesn't fly as high, but flies longer, descends symmetrically.
3. Rises even more slowly, even lower, even longer, descends symmetrically.
4. Rises even more slowly still, flies even lower and longer, but midway through the rise there is a sharp, short spike that is higher than any other point of any of the other types.

I suggest that for a given type of task, each participant, other things being equal, will show a curve of one type.

What about other types of tasks? Generally speaking — a different type. But since we have at most two active codings, there are also at most two possible warm-up curves for those codings. But no fewer than that, either.

## Most-of-all

At the start of the work, type 1 has the fastest rise, so it is the fastest.

Type 3 has the longest plateau at a constant height, so it is the most enduring.

Type 1 has the lowest activity curve, so it makes less noise than the others and can hear what noise the others are making, while also being able to respond to external events rather than merely know about them, since its peak of capacity allows it to perform an unexpected act.

Type 2 is energetic but does not aspire to leadership (that is claimed by type 1), so it helps many people (type 4 helps few), and generally behaves in a friendly way.

## Aggression–depression

The first type is excessively energetic; even when it tries to help others, it does so at too fast a pace, which is hard for others. This is perceived as aggression.

Type 4 is constantly in an energy deficit, which is perceived as depression.
