It is hard to study a living person. It is hard to discuss people who are little known. But it is convenient to discuss public figures who have died. It is even more convenient to discuss classic writers — they themselves put their thoughts and emotions on display.

Try to identify a single temperament in Pushkin, Gogol, or Leo Tolstoy. You won't manage it. But if we assume that one person has two temperaments, one of which dominates, the problem is solved fairly simply.

![[Pasted image 20260714094254.png]]But sometimes we have to assume the presence of three temperaments.

For example, I see Lermontov at times as (sanguine choleric), at times as (melancholic choleric). The first Lermontov is merciless, the second is sensitive and even prone to tears.
