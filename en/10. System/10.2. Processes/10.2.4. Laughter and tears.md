No one before me has treated laughter and tears as an energetic phenomenon, and tears in particular have never really been analyzed. The various accounts of laughter (Aristotle, Freud, and others) are not sufficient.

Here is what we know about these phenomena:

|       | relaxation | energy               | assessment of the world |
| ----- | ---------- | -------------------- | ------------------------ |
| laughter | yes     | in surplus            | positive                 |
| tears    | yes     | in deep deficit       | negative                  |

Imagine that the brain, before setting out to solve a problem, accumulates energy. And in the course of solving it, this energy is spent.![[Pasted image 20260714093114.png]]

Then laughter and tears can be decoded as follows:
LAUGHTER
![[Pasted image 20260714093230.png]]TEARS
![[Pasted image 20260714093307.png]]
Discovering midway through solving a problem that it will require more energy than we have or will have within a reasonable time, we give up on solving it. The result is tears.

In summary:

**Laughter:**

1. a lot of energy had been accumulated
2. the task was expected to require a large expenditure, but it turned out to be small
3. the task got solved, and there was extra energy left over — **success**

**Tears:**

1. The accumulated energy was not enough (and it has all been spent), and there is nowhere to get more.
2. The task is taken off the agenda — **failure.**
3. The energy was spent in vain, it could have been spent usefully.

### To these two phenomena we can add one more —

**Procrastination:**

1. accumulation proceeds SLOWLY
2. the task requires a LARGE expenditure
3. long preparation is needed

### Now we are ready to expand the list of phenomena to ten items.

1. Procrastination
2. Laughter
3. Tears
4. Depression
5. Aggression
6. Sensitivity
7. Friendliness
8. Endurance
9. Humor
10. Sarcasm

Let's use another analogy (instead of energy and water): a student receives pocket money from their mother in the morning before school, to be spent later in the school cafeteria. Four students take part in our reasoning: Pete (the first), Vic (the second), Tony (the third), and Charles (the fourth).

Pete is from a well-off family — he is given as much money for school as he asks for. So every day he buys himself a pastry for 6 rubles. Tony is from a poor family. He is given 1 ruble a day. A bun costs 1 ruble. But Tony saves his money and buys himself a pastry once a week. Charles's parents are odd — sometimes they give him a lot of money, sometimes none at all.

- **Procrastination.** Tony saves money all week.
- **Endurance.** Tony is enduring.
- **Tears.** Charles got 100 rubles in the morning, but lost all the money on the way.
- **Laughter.** Pete thought the pastry cost 100 rubles, asked his parents for 100 rubles, and got it. He paid only 6 rubles for the pastry — he both ate the pastry and kept almost all the money!
- **Depression.** After Charles admitted at home that he lost the money on the way, he was punished — he will get nothing at all for the whole next week.
- **Aggression.** Pete brought 100 rubles, but the cafeteria lady was sick and the cafeteria was closed — Pete furiously pounds on the door.
- **Sarcasm.** Pete bought a pastry and got 94 rubles in change. Now he is eating it — and taunting Tony and Charles, who want to buy a pastry but have no money.
- **Humor.** Vic gets 8 rubles every morning, spends six on a pastry, and gives the two rubles in change to Tony and Charles. He jokes not about others but about himself.
- **Friendliness.** Vic is friendly; he resembles all the others at once.
- **Sensitivity.** Charles does not save money for later. If it turns out he has extra money left, he readily gives it away to those in need.
