Théodule Ribot, from the book *Psychology of Attention*, Moscow, 2005, p. 332, Ribot's 1892 article. It cites a study from 1885.

"The most recent studies mentioned above have shown that the word does not exist in the same form in all people. For some, it consists mainly of moments of articulation. Stricker, in his book on speech and music, drawing on personal experience, described a complete type of such subjects; this is a predominantly motor type. For others, the word consists mainly of auditory images; this is the inner speech so well described by V. Egger. Still others, much rarer, think in words that are read or written. This is the visual type. In most people all these elements act in unequal doses. But always and everywhere, whether a word is spoken aloud or is a purely internal sign, it rests on some form of original perception, and therefore contains motor elements. There is no doubt that the motor elements contained in general ideas of any category are often very weak. This agrees with the fact, established by experience, that abstract thinking is impossible for many and difficult for almost everyone."

(further — a footnote on p. 333) "We said that the study of a large number of cases, both normal and pathological, led to the recognition of several types: motor, auditory, and visual, depending on the group of images that predominates in each individual (leaving aside the ordinary or indifferent type). One who thinks by pronouncing words but not hearing them (Stricker), one who thinks by hearing words but not pronouncing them (V. Egger), and finally one who thinks by seeing words written, without hearing or pronouncing them, represent root types, incommensurable with one another. This at once excludes any possibility of dispute. Each is right with respect to himself and those like him; he is wrong only if he generalizes without limits.

It would be desirable for the work done on images and various forms of speech to be undertaken for general ideas as well. It is possible that root types would be found there too. For instance, it seems to me that in Berkeley general ideas take a visual form. Anyone who attentively reads certain passages of his famous treatise on human nature (too long to quote here), and studies them not as a theory of general ideas but as a document and a psychological confession, will conclude that for him the general idea was a vision. "The idea of man," he says, "which I am able to fabricate for myself, must be the idea of a man white, black, or swarthy, straight, stooped, tall, small, or of average height. No effort of thought allows me to grasp the abstract idea described above (that is, the idea of a color that is neither red, nor blue, nor green, etc., but is still a color)." On the other hand, it seems to me that among the nominalists general ideas take a purely auditory form.

The famous theory that reduced universals to pure "flatus vocis" (Roscelin, Hobbes, etc.) allows, in my view, two interpretations. Taken literally, it is nonsense. A pure "flatus vocis" is a word belonging to a language entirely unknown, which for that reason is associated with no idea and remains merely a sound or noise. It is unlikely that reasonable thinkers defended this thesis in the form usually attributed to them. I propose another interpretation.

The nominalists are dry, algebraic minds, content with the word alone, which awakens no image. For them no representation exists except sound. We have moved very far from Berkeley.

Stricker, who cannot think a single word without pronouncing it, a purely motor type, as far as possible from the auditory type, tells us: "I must attach something to every word so that it does not remain a dead term to me, like a word from a language I do not know. When words such as 'immortality' or 'virtue' come to mind on various occasions in life, I explain them to myself not through words but through visual images. At the word virtue I think of some woman's face; at the word 'courage' — of an armed man, and so on."

This mode of representing abstract and general ideas might be called the antipode of nominalism. In medicine it is said that there are no diseases, only patients; likewise there are no general ideas, only minds that think differently. Instead of a philosophical approach — that is, the drive to reduce everything to unity — it is time to take up the psychological approach, that is, to identify the principal types. Then, no doubt, many disputes would cease of their own accord. In any case, it seems to me that this work is worth undertaking."
