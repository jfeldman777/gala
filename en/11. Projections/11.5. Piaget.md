
Conservation laws.
In a series of experiments, Piaget showed that 5-year-olds and 8-year-olds understand physical processes differently. Here are descriptions of two experiments.

First.
Buttons lie on a table in two parallel rows.
The spacing in each row is the same, and it is clear that there are equal numbers of buttons.

Examiner: Equal?
Child: Equal.

The examiner spreads out the first row of buttons.

Examiner:
And now — where are there more?
Child: Here (points to the first row)

Second.
The examiner pours water into two tall, narrow glasses.

Examiner:
Equal?
Child: Equal.

Then the examiner pours the water from the first tall glass into a wide glass.
Naturally, the water level in the wide glass is lower than in the second tall one.

Examiner: and now — where is there more?
Child: more here (points to the narrow glass)

That was a 5-year-old. Put an 8-year-old in the same place, and in both cases the child will answer: equal.

Piaget explains: at 5 a person does not yet understand the laws of conservation. At 8 — they do.
Why?
Piaget explains:
At 8 the child can take several factors into account, and at 5 — only one factor.
For example, comparing water in a narrow and a wide glass, one must see that the height became smaller and the cross-sectional area — larger.

My comment. Piaget gives not one but two whole explanations; that is a sign he is not sure the arguments are right.

Indeed, simply knowing that "the height became smaller and the cross-section larger" is not enough to claim the volume did not change.

And what is enough?
And why does the 5-year-old not have it?

My answer. The 5-year-old thinks the world as many objects; the 8-year-old — as a continuous process.
In a continuous process, if there are no clear signs of Income or Expense, the quantity is taken as unchanged. If there is no continuous line, only many frames and many objects, what catches the eye first is counted. Here — the height of water in the glass. Or the length of the button row
