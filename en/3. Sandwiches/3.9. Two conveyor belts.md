Rutherford's method — bombard something in order to understand what's inside — seems universal to me. I myself was under such fire when I took oral exams (there used to be such things, in the pre-computer era).

It's like a machine feeding balls to train tennis players. It's also like Ford's assembly line, where parts arrive to you at a constant rhythm.

At the same time, once it reaches a student, a task passes through a certain processing cycle of its own. A special, internal conveyor belt moves the task from one agent to another.

Who are these agents?

Psychologists study them separately, as mental faculties. I've tried to reconstruct how this internal conveyor belt works. As far as I know, no one has ever described problem-solving quite this way.

For now I'll just list the agents. I'll describe their functions and interdependencies later.
So. Involved in solving tasks are:
1. Emotions
2. Will
3. Attention
4. Encodings
5. Warm-up
6. Memory
7. Combinatorial intelligence
8. Creative intelligence
9. Intuition
