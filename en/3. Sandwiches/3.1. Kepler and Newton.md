In 1971 I finished eighth grade in the city of Odessa and was admitted (competitively, through an interview) to class 9V (the math class) of School No. 116 (there was such a school for older students — just two eighth grades, six ninth grades, and six tenth grades).

Our physics teacher was the wonderful Yakov Petrovich Tsikinovsky. Wonderful for two reasons. First, he was redheaded, like a true Irishman. Second, he talked like Ekaterina Schulmann, only faster.

It was Yakov Petrovich who pointed out to us that in physics there are laws of two types. Some laws give you a formula derived from numerical data. Hooke's Law, for instance.
Others give you a formula derived from theoretical principles. For example, Einstein's formula relating Energy and Mass.

But the most important moment in physical understanding is when laws of the first type meet laws of the second type and form a closed construction — a Bingo (as Yakov Petrovich put it), or a Sandwich, as I now call it.

My favorite sandwich in physics is how Kepler's laws met Newton's laws.
Second example — Bohr's postulates and the spectral line series of the hydrogen atom.
Third example — the periodic table and the Schrödinger equation (accounting for the Pauli exclusion principle).

Molecules of understanding like these, once born, don't die.
