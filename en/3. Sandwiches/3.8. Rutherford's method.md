Ernest Rutherford was a model experimental physicist.
I'll briefly tell you about his two discoveries, but I'll start with a tale.

(A "tale" here is an anecdote based on real events. A ready-made story with no known author. A script that reveals a meaning.)

So, around 1923, the future academician Kapitsa traveled to England and arrived early one morning at Rutherford's laboratory. "What do you want?" Rutherford asked the young man.
"I want to work for you," the future academician answered the current Nobel laureate.

"But I've already hired my staff," said Rutherford, "and I don't need any more."

"May I ask two questions before I go?"
"Before you go — you may."

"How many lab assistants do you have in your lab?"
"Thirty-three."

"And the second question. What margin of error is considered acceptable in your results?"
"Three percent."

They were silent for a moment.
"All right," said Rutherford, "come in tomorrow morning. We'll get to work."

Rutherford made his first major discovery in 1903. He was studying the radioactive emissions of uranium. The radiation was absorbed by a lead shell, so that a directed beam emerged from a hole in the shell and exposed a spot on a photographic plate. That was before the experiment. But during the experiment, Rutherford placed the beam in a strong magnetic field.
And the beam split into three. Straight ahead went the radio waves that we now know as gamma rays.
To the left, with a slight deflection, went the positive alpha particles. Today we know these are helium nuclei without electrons, giving the particle a charge of 2+. The fact that they deflected only slightly tells us they have a relatively large mass.

To the right, deflecting quite strongly (small mass), went the negatively charged beta particles. Today we know these are electrons. The terms alpha, beta, and gamma radiation are still used today when talking about radioactive damage or contamination.

In his second famous experiment, in 1911, Rutherford isolated a stream of alpha particles and directed it at a thin sheet of gold foil. Why gold? Gold is a soft metal; it can be rolled out so thin that the layer is just one molecule thick.

His lab assistants discovered two phenomena, each more interesting than the last.

The first phenomenon — almost all the particles passed straight through the foil, as light passes through clear glass. The second phenomenon — one particle in 8,000 bounced straight back, like a tennis ball off a wall. What does this tell us?

1) almost all the space between the centers of atoms is empty.
2) small but heavy atomic nuclei are positively charged and are able to deflect an alpha particle if it hits them.
