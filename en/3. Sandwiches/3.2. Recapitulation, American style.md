This word was introduced into science by Granville Stanley Hall, the first professor of psychology in the US, the man who invited Freud and Jung to lecture in the US at the same time (even though the two had already fallen out by then). (Photo from 1909, front row: Hall in the center, Freud on the left, Jung on the right.)

Hall was the first person on the American continent to take up child and developmental psychology, and he realized the topic had no philosophical foundation.
So, looking around,
he recalled the controversy around Darwin,
the biogenetic law
(that an individual organism, in the womb, passes through the history of its species),
and formulated a sociogenetic law:
a human being, from birth to social maturity, passes through the stages that humanity passed through, from the moment it separated from the animal world to the present day.

The word "recapitulation" itself refers us to this sociogenetic law.

I should note right away that Hall did not try to organize the educational process according to the tenets of his theory. Quite the opposite — he spoke to educators, urging them to study child psychology.

Here is how Hall's views on children are described by his student Hutchinson:

1) birth – 5 years – the digging and burrowing stage (playing with sand, manipulating a bucket and a little shovel);

2) 5 – 11 years – the hunting and capturing stage (fear of strangers, displays of aggression and cruelty, a desire to fence oneself off from adults, and an urge to conceal things);

3) 8 – 12 years – the pastoral stage (a desire to have one's own corner, a passion for hideouts (mainly in yards, in fields, in forests, but not in the house), a love of pets and a desire to have them, care for them, a craving for affection and tenderness (especially in girls));

4) 11 – 15 years – the agricultural stage (interest in nature, weather, gardening, floriculture, the emergence of observational skill and caution);

5) 14 – 20 years – the stage of industry and trade, or the stage of the modern person (awareness of the role of money, the significance of the exact sciences, a desire to exchange things);

Hutchinson, following Hall, believed that starting from the pastoral stage (8–12 years), the era of the civilized human begins, and it is from this age that children are psychologically ready for systematic instruction, which isn't fully possible at earlier stages of a child's psychological development.

It's a good thing I didn't know any of this when I arrived at the idea of recapitulation.
