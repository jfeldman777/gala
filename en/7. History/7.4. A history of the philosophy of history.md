### A short course in the history of the philosophy of history (hussars, keep quiet!)

In the history of philosophy there have been many attempts to make sense of the history of humankind.

The Greeks and Romans thought of the world locally; for them near history was the account of eyewitnesses, and distant history was myth.

In the Middle Ages the Christian Church understood the history of humankind as “what is described in the Bible.”

The French Revolution cast doubt on the truth of all conceptions tied to the old regime, including the whole church ideology.

The first University of the new type appeared — Berlin, 1806.

Philosophers appeared ready to make sense of the history of humankind “not according to the Bible,” and the first of them — Hegel (1770–1831). Hegel received the chair of philosophy at the University of Berlin (1818).

After Hegel, Marx came to Earth (1818–1883).

Then came Spengler (1880–1936), and after him — Toynbee (1889–1975).

The last two rejected history-of-humankind-as-one-process and pictured the history of humankind “à la Darwin” — as a struggle of civilization-organisms.

Then there were two world wars and the Iron Curtain. When the Iron Curtain fell, Francis Fukuyama (b. 1952) came to Earth with the book “The End of History and the Last Man” (1992)

Fukuyama tried to combine “history as process” and “history as a struggle of organisms.” Roughly thus: capitalism and communism fought. Capitalism won; the process reached its logical end. But about ten years passed — and it turned out that no, it had not.

Instead came Yuval Noah Harari (b. 1976) and Steven Pinker (b. 1954).

Pinker published a book where he proved with numbers that we become kinder literally every ten years. And Harari gathered the newest data of historians and archaeologists and showed how in this process the global connectedness of humankind arises.

Once more from the beginning.

Does history have a direction? Can one speak of progress, of development, of movement from worse to better?

The Bible: “we are moving toward the Last Judgment and the Resurrection of the Dead.”

Hegel: “reasonableness increases” (by the way, who knows what that is?)

Marx: “we are moving toward a society without classes” (and the Resurrection of the Dead)

Spengler and Toynbee: “we are treading water; there is no progress.”

Fukuyama: “I thought that Good conquers Evil, that humankind becomes better, but I was wrong; nothing changes.”

Pinker: “It does change! We grow kinder!”

Harari: “the global connectedness of the world increases — that is progress”

We: “reasonableness increases, that is complexity, that is the level of TUAI.”

(Hegel: “wow!”; the rest fall silent in confusion).
