Hegel everywhere insists that he works with concepts that continually move, develop, and turn into something else.
Fortunately, this claim (call it the postulate of the movement of concepts) can be taken out of brackets, and what remains is a developed and rather convenient network of concepts.

As for the postulate of the movement of concepts, it was very precisely mocked by the writer Lewis Carroll (who was also professor of mathematical logic Charles Dodgson), in the scene where Alice plays croquet using a flamingo instead of a mallet and hedgehogs instead of balls. It is perfectly clear that one cannot play that way.

Why did Hegel need this postulate of movement?
I have two hypotheses.

First. Young Hegel was indelibly impressed by Napoleon’s invasion of Prussia, when the world’s stability vanished and life began to drift.

Second. Since each discourse has its own logic, and in an excursus they mix and mutually destroy each other, some substitute for logic is needed — and that is what Mr. Hegel offers under the name of dialectical logic, entirely in the spirit of the croquet mentioned above
