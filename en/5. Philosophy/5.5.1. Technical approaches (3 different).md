
1)      Usually the efficiency–morality dilemma surfaces. The case becomes hard, however, when the ratio of output indicators differs under different planning horizons (in time and in social ties).

If, by widening the horizon, we are forced to declare formerly optimal decisions non-optimal, then the right horizon must be chosen.

The general rule is this: in the limit, morality and efficiency coincide.

How far the horizon should be pushed — that is a separate problem and is solved in its own way in each separate case

2)      One can treat a complex choice point as a multicriteria optimization task. Von Neumann advised splitting the list of criteria into two: drive one into constraints, fold the other into a single function, monotonic in many parameters. What is in the constraints is, by definition, morality. What is folded is, by definition, efficiency  
  

3)      One can treat optimization separately from each level. Then the contribution of the odd levels can be treated as efficiency, and the contribution of the even ones as morality. It is important that after every odd level of consideration there follow the next even, higher one.
