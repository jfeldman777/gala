A **philosophy textbook** defines philosophy as knowledge of / the search for the most general laws of everything.  
**Socrates**: philosophy is the art of asking questions  
**Bertrand Russell**. When science and art have given up on a problem, they hand it to the philosophers.  
**Confucius**. I would say to the ruler: begin by correcting the names.  
**Early Wittgenstein**: if you have stumbled on a problem that cannot be solved,  
the language in which you formulate your problem is to blame.  
**Late Wittgenstein**. People fail to understand one another because of the different contexts sitting in their heads. Philosophers, study contexts  
**Camus**. Philosophy should help a person survive.

Before us are definitions of philosophy given from within philosophy itself.  
Are any of them the same? Yes. Confucius coincides with early Wittgenstein. Both claim that philosophy should repair language.

Are the remaining six definitions distinct?  
Yes.  
Are definitions other than these possible?  
No.  
We will show this

Consider an abstract situation — a confrontation of two sides. Let the order of the sides not matter. The sides inside the situation may be of the same type.  
And let the sides come in three types. Into how many types does such a situation break down?  
The correct answer is **six**.
I will not give a strict proof, but simply list the variants.  
AA, AB, AC, BB, CC, BC

Imagine each of the six situations and try to see which definition of philosophy fits each of these six situations.

The situation **thing against thing**. Of course here one must think of a world that  
is deterministic, governed by laws, and it would be good to understand those laws. There is no human here. There are no human groups; no one breaks the laws. Things obey the laws. Therefore studying the laws makes sense.

**Second situation:** human against things. Of course such a human must ask questions of things, and things will answer — that is how new knowledge appears. We have landed in Socrates’ definition.

**Third situation**: human against a community or society. Society presses on this person and they must survive. Here Camus’ definition appears: a person must survive.

Next, the **fourth definition:** group against group. The most important groups are science, art, and philosophy. And here we land in the definition given by Bertrand Russell.

Two situations remain.  
**Fifth situation.** Human against human. Here we land in late Wittgenstein’s definition. The main problem is communication.

And finally **sixth situation**. Community against things. For such a community to work it needs a shared language. That is why the problem of the right language arises, and philosophy must correct that language. So we land in the definition of Confucius and early Wittgenstein.

In effect we have six different philosophies. They differ by their main frame.
