I go out into the yard with my grandson. (A true story, repeated many times with different grandchildren.)
Children are playing in the yard, roughly 3 years old. Each is holding their own toy.
I want to play with that little car, my grandson tells me — he's already been taught not to grab other people's things.
Then offer him yours, I say.
Why?
If he takes yours, he'll let go of his own, and then you'll be able to play with it.

The scheme works in 90% of cases. The other 10% is the case of "a toy just bought" — the first love hasn't worn off yet.

Let's try to break down what's described above.
If this were music, this would be called "write down the melody in musical notation."

I want — one object in the field of attention — the first level.
I want (but you can't just grab it) — allowed/not-allowed, many objects — the second level.

Here's a sequence of actions for you:
1. Offer yours.
2. Wait until he lets go of his grip on his own and grabs yours.
3. Take it and play.

Carrying out the sequence — the third level.
Teaching the sequence of actions — the fourth level.

The child pushes the odd levels (I want, I carry out the plan).
The caregiver holds the even levels (allowed/not-allowed, I teach what's right).
