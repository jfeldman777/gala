What happens during an ordinary lesson at an ordinary school?

1) The teacher is in the classroom, the students are in the classroom, the lesson has begun.
2) The teacher shows a problem. The teacher shows the solution.
3) The teacher shows a new problem of the same class.
4) A student comes to the board, solves the problem while looking at the model example.
5) Everyone else watches and, if possible, copies it into their notebook.
6) The cycle repeats from step 3 for as long as there is time and there are still students who haven't had their turn, or until one of those runs out first.

Which levels are engaged here?

Recognition: a problem from the named class.
We go through all the elements of the problem — matching them with the elements of the model problem.
Using this correspondence, we repeat the teacher's actions step by step.

So which levels are used here?

The first (recognition).
The second (going through a finite set of elements).
The third (step-by-step execution of a linear sequence of operations).

And is that all? That's all.

Is that bad? Bad.
Why? Because abilities arise from being called upon — and die from not being called upon.

If abilities above the third level are not trained, they simply won't arise. And, among others, these include:
No. 4 — cooperation with equals within a role system
No. 5 — making independent decisions
No. 6 — debriefing
