![[тофлер.png]]Alvin Toffler used the best possible template for his books. His two best-known books, "Future Shock" (1970) and "The Third Wave" (1980), are world bestsellers for good reason. That's how every good author should do it. One metaphor per book — put in the title and on the cover — and the whole book is a set of examples showing why that metaphor makes sense.

"Future Shock" alludes to a term popular in the '70s and now forgotten: "culture shock." You emigrate to another country, unexpectedly, for political or other reasons — you find yourself in a new cultural environment, and you get a shock — a culture shock.

Toffler drew attention to how quickly life was changing in the US (recall, this was 1970) — you didn't go anywhere, but the ground moved out from under your feet — that's future shock.

In "The Third Wave," Toffler draws the reader's attention to the fact that a lifestyle is a whole, integral set of things and scenarios that is contagious, like a virus — neighbors pick up the style quickly, and it sweeps across the planet like a wave.

This describes the historical process better than, say, Marx's socioeconomic formations. In Marx, the whole country moves to a new stage all at once, once it has accumulated enough features of the new. With Toffler, things are more nuanced. Waves can converge in a single room, where, at three computers standing side by side, one employee is modeling the Universe, another is doing the accounting, and a third is playing a farming game.

For Toffler, the first wave was farmers, the second the industrial age, the third the information age. After the book, a zeroth wave appeared — hunters (Toffler agreed with this addition). These four waves in the picture above map onto the scale of levels.
