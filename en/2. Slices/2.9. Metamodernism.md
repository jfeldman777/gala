The start of the 20th century — an explosive growth of everything: science, art, technology, invention. Novelty is valuable in itself. But that's true for the creative 20%. For the remaining 80%, a growing desire builds to stop this damned motion that we can't keep up with!

And since the ruling classes (above all in Russia and Germany) fell not into the creative 20% but into the discontented 80%, a war began. The First World War.

The new ideas that flourished at the start of the 20th century were given the name Modernism (which set them against Tradition — the New against the Old).

But the positive energy of the first half of the 20th century, colliding with the mass movements of the First and Second World Wars, ran dry, and the children of the modernists became the Postmodernists.

"Your creativity nearly destroyed civilization," they told their fathers, "we won't be adding new ideas to your results — we're going to critique and revise them instead."

"Your ideas didn't save the world, didn't make it happy, and nearly reduced it to zero. Let's take a closer look."

That's how Postmodernism appeared.

The children of the modernists, who glorified reason, took up the destruction of earlier templates and results. Perhaps in Europe and America this made sense — that's not for us to judge — but in the Russian-speaking world it was time lost.

I belong to the movement that came after postmodernism — metamodernism.

If Modernism raised Authorship as the highest value, and Postmodernism threw Authorship overboard from the ship of modernity, Metamodernism takes a Third position.

"Humanity has many shared tasks," say the metamodernists. "Let's settle it through glory, since we're all in this together — the main thing is the task; there are enough shared tasks for all of humanity to go around for everyone."

This very attitude toward practical tasks needed by many gives rise to various ideas — for example, Rule 3, Rule 5, and Rule 8 of the eight rules of fruitful discussion.
