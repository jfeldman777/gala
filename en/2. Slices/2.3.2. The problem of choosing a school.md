Imagine. You're a parent, your child is school-age. You and your family have moved (to another city, another country, another continent). Now you need to choose housing for the family and a school for the child.

In the US, a child's school is determined by the parents' place of residence, so I chose the school first and the apartment second.

Suppose you don't trust other people on this question and want to make a well-founded decision yourself.

What do I recommend in this case?

Take a sheet of paper, a pencil, and write down, in a column, which abilities you want to develop in your child.

It can be anything at all.
From knowing the multiplication table and being able to solve partial differential equations — to the ability to never be late, to keep one's word, and to be the life of any gathering.

Written it out?

Now agree with the principal and get permission to come to the school for a month and sit in on any lesson.

Go to the school as if it were a job.
While doing so, glance at your list of abilities. And every time an ability is called upon, put a checkmark.

After a month, look at the list and cross out every ability that was called upon even once.

What's left?
If the list is empty, feel free to choose this school.
If the list isn't empty, then for each remaining ability you must pick one of three options:
1) This ability isn't essential — cross it out.
2) This ability is essential, but we'll arrange to train it outside of school.
3) Neither of the above — you need to look for a different school.

At the very least, it will be an honest, conscious decision.
