Euclid is the most famous mathematician in the history of humanity. For many, he's still the standard of scientific rigor to this day. Spinoza's famous Ethics was written using Euclid's method (Axioms, Definitions, Theorems) — the first case I know of where a mathematical method was used to investigate and present human problems.

David Hilbert was a leading German mathematician who worked at the turn of the 19th–20th centuries.

His slim book "Foundations of Geometry" is unknown to the general reader.
And yet, sewn into it is a new style of mathematical thinking — one that isn't taught in school (while Euclid is).

So what's the difference?

Euclid.
A point is that which has no parts. A line is length without width. And then — a diagram.
Axiom. Through any two distinct points, one can always draw a line, and only one.

Hilbert. Let us consider entities of two kinds. Entities of the first kind we will call lines, and denote with lowercase English letters — a, b, c, ...

Entities of the second kind we will call points, and denote with uppercase English letters — A, B, C, ...

Between lines and points there exists a relation "lies on."
This means the question "does X lie on x" always has meaning and a value. The value can be true or false (but not both at once).

Axiom: there are two points A, B, and two lines a, b.
A lies on a
B lies on b
A lies on b
B lies on a
Then — if A and B are distinct, then
a and b are the same.

I draw your attention to the fact that understanding Euclid requires intuitive illustrations, while understanding Hilbert doesn't.

So what is a point and a line, according to Hilbert?
They are entities of two kinds, denoted as above, and satisfying the Axioms (see the example above).

Hilbert called this kind of definition axiomatic. I call it inverse.
