The Hungarian-American Thomas S. Kuhn published a book in 1962 called "The Structure of Scientific Revolutions."

The book became a classic right away, and here's why.

Kuhn separated Normal Science from Scientific Revolution.

Most of the time, the scientific community exists in a state of Normal Science. In this state, ordinary scientists are engaged in an ordinary process — solving puzzles.

Puzzles — Kuhn's term — correspond roughly to the semi-open problems of the sixth level. But besides that, in the course of their work scientists also record anomalies, which get set aside for a while.

At some point there get to be too many anomalies. Within the terms of the old paradigm (Kuhn's term), they can't be solved.

The term Paradigm can be replaced with framework, or with Discourse. I prefer the latter term.

A paradigm sets not just priorities, not just what counts as a legitimate move in an experiment or a proof, but everything that science rests on.

So, there are too many anomalies. They can't be solved within the old paradigm. Some scientists switch to searching for a new paradigm.

This is how a scientific revolution begins. At some point, new paradigms start pulling in scientists from other paradigms. This process was vividly described by Imre Lakatos in his response to Kuhn's book.

The revolution ends with the final victory of one of the paradigms. And gradually, normal science begins there again.

Now, the levels.

Normal science — level 6
A new paradigm — level 7
Analysis of competing paradigms — level 8.

Kuhn and Lakatos demonstrate level 8.
This was — as far as I know — the first legitimization of level 8 in history.
